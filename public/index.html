Generating proteins by learning backbones and latents

An interactive visualization project by Jasper Butcher

A scroll-driven tour of partially-latent diffusion - a recent paradigm for designing new proteins, where a generative model learns to draw the backbone in 3D and to encode each amino-acid side chain as a compact eight-number “fingerprint”.

About this project

Purpose. Partially-latent diffusion is a recent paradigm for protein design that sidesteps the discrete combinatorics of amino acids by encoding each side chain into a continuous eight-dimensional vector z. A single diffusion model then operates over backbone coordinates and these z’s simultaneously - generating an entire protein in one shot rather than the traditional backbone-first, sequence-second pipeline. The technique works, but the latent space it builds is opaque: developers have no easy way to ask “what does the model actually think about a given residue?” without training expensive downstream evaluators. This tool exists to fill that gap - to give newcomers a concrete tour of how partially-latent diffusion works, and practitioners a fast visual way to probe the quality of any trained latent.

Visual encodings. The page is structured as three linked views over the same model. A 3D protein viewer (NGL) shows forward-noising on a chosen hero structure, paired with a log-scale σ–τ schedule plot that anchors the noise level quantitatively as you scroll. A 2D t-SNE atlas of decoded residues uses five interchangeable color encodings to ask different questions of the same point cloud - a categorical paper palette for the 20 amino acids, and a continuous crest ramp for hydrophobicity, heavy-atom count, rotamer strain, and latent norm ‖z‖. Hovering any dot decodes that latent into a real side chain rendered in a small NGL viewer, and projects its torsions onto a χ-distribution grid whose ±60°/±180° axes act as reference frames for ideal rotamer wells. Backbone, latent, atoms, and dihedrals are all visible - and linked - at once.

Noising the latents, not the atoms

Diffusion models generate by learning to undo noise: a network is trained to remove a little Gaussian blur from real data, and those denoising steps are chained together to draw new samples from scratch. The catch for proteins is that atoms are discrete - you can’t smoothly “noise” a carbon into a nitrogen without breaking chemistry.

Partially-latent diffusion sidesteps this by noising the backbone coordinates and the per-residue latent z separately. Both are continuous; both deform smoothly. Below, the noise level σ grows with diffusion time τ as the protein blurs into Gaussian fog. Toggle between a classic diffusion schedule and a newer flow-matching one to see how the same protein is corrupted along two different paths.

What does the latent space actually look like?

The autoencoder squeezes every side chain into the same eight-dimensional space - but is that space actually meaningful? Below, a few thousand decoded residues are projected onto a 2D map using t-SNE, a technique that flattens high-dimensional points so chemically similar ones land near each other.

Hover any dot to see the side chain it decodes to, its rotamer torsions, and its latent energy ‖z‖. Switch the coloring to recolor by amino-acid type, hydrophobicity, heavy-atom count, rotamer strain, or latent norm - each lens exposes a different way the model has organized its eight numbers.

Clean gradients are a good sign the latent has learned real chemistry; messy clusters tell you where the model still has work to do.
