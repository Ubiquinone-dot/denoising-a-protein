Noising and Denoising Proteins

How RFdiffusion learns to design proteins by being trained to un-noise them.

Most explainers of protein-design diffusion models show inference: a trained model takes a cloud of noise and turns it into a protein. But that's not how the model is taught. During training, real protein structures are noised forward, and the model is asked to predict the clean version.

The recipe for which atoms to noise is what makes the same network capable of unconditional design, motif scaffolding, binder design, and more. There is no "binder head" or "motif head" — just a mask over the input.

A different kind of noise: flow matching

EDM's σ schedule is unbounded — by the time τ = 1, atoms have drifted thousands of Ångstroms from their crystal positions. That profligate variance is what gives diffusion models their expressive training distribution, but it's also why their inference is a multi-hundred-step climb back down the schedule.

Flow matching takes a tidier route. Sample a noise cloud once (mean zero, fixed variance σ² = 12² Å²), then define a straight line in coordinate space from the clean structure at t = 0 to the noise cloud at t = 1. No schedule. No exploding tails. The model is trained to predict the constant velocity field that traces that line. The same three masking recipes — unconditional, motif scaffolding, binder design — apply unchanged.

What you just saw

All three panels share the same EDM noise schedule (Karras et al. 2022), with parameters borrowed from RFD3: σdata=16, σmin=4×10−4, σmax=160, ρ=7. The only thing that differs between panels is a binary mask: which atoms are allowed to drift, and which are pinned to their crystal positions.

That single insight — conditioning = masking — is the engine behind RFdiffusion's flexibility. Train one model on a mixture of all three regimes, and at inference time you can run it in any of them just by choosing the mask.
